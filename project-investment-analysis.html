Task B — Analytical Essays

Interest Rate Policy & Deposit Insurance

B.1 — Interest Rate Transmission Mechanism

Central banks use various tools to manage the economy. The most powerful of these is the discount rate. The Central Bank of Azerbaijan (CBAR) uses this tool to directly influence the behaviour of commercial banks and, indirectly, the deposit income of the population. This essay explains the mechanism of that influence, real examples from Azerbaijan, and the weaknesses of the system in sequence.

The discount rate is the price at which the central bank lends to commercial banks. When this rate rises, borrowing from the central bank becomes more expensive for commercial banks. They therefore turn to an alternative source — the deposits of the public. To attract more deposits, they raise their deposit rates. In this way, a rise in the discount rate triggers a chain reaction that also raises deposit rates. The reverse is also true. When the discount rate falls, banks can borrow cheaply from the central bank. The need for public deposits decreases, and deposit rates fall. In economics, this process is called the interest rate transmission mechanism. A central bank decision affects the entire banking system.

In 2022, annual inflation in Azerbaijan reached 13.85%. This rapid rise in prices forced CBAR to raise its discount rate. Banks in turn raised their deposit rates. That period played a key role in shaping the 10–12% deposit rates we see in the market today.

By 2024, inflation had fallen sharply to 2.21%. CBAR began lowering its rate. However, commercial banks did not lower their deposit rates at the same pace. The reason is simple: banks do not want to lose customers in a competitive environment. Suddenly cutting rates could lead to customer losses. This is what economists call "asymmetric transmission" — banks react quickly when rates go up, but slowly when they come down.

There are three main reasons why this mechanism does not work with full efficiency in Azerbaijan.

The first is high dollarisation. A portion of the population still holds their savings in dollars or euros. For these people, changes in deposit rates set in manats are not very significant. This is why CBAR's rate decisions on the manat do not affect every member of society.

The second is that state banks reduce competition. State banks like Kapital Bank and ABB know they have government backing. This sense of security sometimes leads them to react more slowly to market signals. The less competition there is, the weaker the transmission mechanism works.

The third is the influence of oil revenues on the interest rate channel. Because Azerbaijan's economy is highly dependent on oil exports, when oil revenues are high, banks have extra resources. At that point, the significance of the central bank's rate changes diminishes, because banks already have sufficient resources.

In Georgia, the transmission mechanism works faster and more transparently. Large private banks such as TBC Bank and Bank of Georgia respond to every rate decision by the National Bank of Georgia within 4–6 weeks and adjust their deposit rates accordingly. Behind this are a strong private competitive environment, transparency requirements from international investors, and declining dollarisation in recent years. This comparison clearly shows that the effectiveness of the transmission mechanism depends not only on the central bank's intentions, but on the overall maturity of the banking sector environment.

Although CBAR's discount rate policy does influence commercial banks' deposit rates to a certain degree, this influence is neither complete nor fast. Dollarisation, state banks, and the oil economy block this mechanism. Compared to Georgia, it is clear that a strong private competitive environment, central bank independence, and currency choices are essential conditions for effective transmission.

B.2 — Deposit Insurance: Protection or Moral Hazard?

The main instrument of legal protection for depositors in Azerbaijan is the Deposit Insurance Fund (ASF/ƏSF). The ASF pays compensation equal to 100% of the insured deposit at each participating bank, provided it does not exceed 100,000 AZN. This limit applies separately for each bank: for example, if the same depositor holds 100,000 AZN in two different banks, both amounts are fully insured. From the day the system was created until 1 January 2025, a total of 1 billion 717 million manats in compensation has been paid to 62,520 depositors across 19 banks under liquidation.

In economic theory, moral hazard describes a situation where one party's protection from the consequences of risk leads that party to behave more recklessly. In the banking context, this manifests in two directions.

From the depositor's side: the existence of insurance frees people from researching a bank's financial health. If every amount up to 100,000 AZN is guaranteed, the customer does not investigate the bank's credit quality or reliability — they simply look for the highest interest rate. This is the main factor that weakens market discipline.

From the bank's side, the situation is more troubling. The bank knows that depositors will not withdraw their money, because insurance exists. At that point, it becomes more profitable for bank management to take excessive risks, because when bets succeed the profit goes to the bank's shareholders, and when they fail, the loss is paid by the ASF — meaning taxpayers bear the cost.

The 100,000 AZN limit is a significant threshold in the context of Azerbaijan's economy. Given the country's average annual wage, this amount allows the vast majority of the population to keep their savings fully insured. The practical result is that almost no depositor evaluates banks sufficiently. The main selection factor in the market becomes only the interest rate. This creates a particular competition model among banks. The bank offering the highest rate attracts more deposits. But to meet the high interest obligation, the bank must direct those funds into more profitable assets — which are naturally riskier.

Taking into account the current structure of the ASF system, the following strategy can be considered optimal for a depositor.

First, apply the principle of portfolio allocation. This means amounts above 100,000 AZN should be held in more than one ASF-member bank so that each portion remains fully insured. This may be the most powerful risk-reduction measure available at the depositor level.

Second, for amounts close to the insurance limit, it is necessary to investigate the bank's financial indicators — in particular, the capital adequacy ratio (CAR) and the liquid assets ratio in the public reports of the Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FIMSA/MBDP) should be monitored.

Third, for amounts exceeding 100,000 AZN, it is more advantageous to choose state-participant banks. For example, the implicit government support present in banks such as Kapital Bank and ABB does not provide a written guarantee, but given that similar situations have occurred in the past, they appear more reliable.

Fourth, one should approach with caution any bank promising rates sharply above the market average. A bank promising 3–4% above the market average is either experiencing a liquidity problem or pursuing an excessively risky lending policy. A high interest rate is not always attractive — sometimes it is a warning signal.

Risk Assessment

⚠ Key Risks

⚠Inflation surge scenario
If inflation rises above 9%, the 12% deposit rate produces a negative real return. This is the primary macro risk — low probability given current AZN stability, but not zero. Monitor CBAR announcements quarterly.
⚠Mutual fund downside scenario
The fund's pessimistic outcome (26,930 AZN gain) is lower than the guaranteed deposit gain (34,355 AZN). Accepting fund risk without sufficient risk tolerance is a strictly worse outcome than the bank deposit on a risk-adjusted basis.
⚠Concentration risk in Leyla's portfolio
Kapital Bank holds 35% of the portfolio (28,000 AZN). Even though it's below the 100k insurance limit, a simultaneous multi-bank crisis (as seen in 2015–16) could delay access to funds even when they're fully insured.
