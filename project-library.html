Objective: Design a fully normalised relational library database in Oracle SQL, populate it with 25 books by 15 classic and contemporary authors, and write 15 analytical queries demonstrating a range of techniques from basic SELECT to correlated subqueries and self-joins.

Key Finding: The collection holds 54 copies across 25 titles and 10 genres, with an overall availability rate of 61%. Mystery and Horror have the lowest availability (25% and 40% respectively), with 3 titles fully checked out. 5 active reservations signal a backlog. Two schema-level defects were identified — a syntax error in the final query and a typo in a CHECK constraint.

Availability & Genre Coverage

Fig 03 — Copies Checked Out (Demand Pressure) per Genre
  • 01Mystery and Horror face the highest demand pressure — with availability rates of 25% and 40% respectively, both genres are undersupplied. Three books in these genres have zero copies left on the shelf.
  • 02Fantasy leads in total copy count (7 across 2 titles). This reflects deliberate acquisition strategy — high-demand titles like Harry Potter justify multiple copies to serve concurrent readers without long wait times.
  • 03Historical Fiction is the most represented genre (4 titles, 7 copies) — spanning Hemingway, Dickens, Tolstoy, and Morrison. Broad literary coverage but uneven availability (War and Peace: 0 copies left).
  • 045 active reservations are queued for unavailable books — The Shining (2 reservations), Orient Express, 1984, and War and Peace each have at least one member waiting, signalling unmet demand.
  • 05The "Other" group (Political Satire, Modernist, Adventure, Sci-Fi) shows 100% availability — all 7 copies of Animal Farm, Mrs Dalloway, Tom Sawyer, and Oryx and Crake are on the shelf, suggesting lower circulation demand.

Query Complexity & Technique Coverage

15 queries were written covering the full spectrum of Oracle SQL — from basic data retrieval to multi-level correlated subqueries and set operations. The chart maps each technique to the number of queries that employ it.

Fig 04 — SQL Techniques: Frequency Across 15 Queries
  • 06Query 7 is the most complex — a correlated subquery inside a WHERE clause. It finds members who borrowed more books than the average, requiring a subquery to compute the average of a subquery: a three-level nested structure.
  • 07Query 14 uses a self-join to find books by the same author — joining the books table to itself on author_id, using a less-than condition on book_id to avoid duplicate pairs. One of the most elegant patterns in relational SQL.

Risk Assessment

⚠ Schema & Data Quality Risks

⚠ No fine or penalty tracking in the loans table
Query 11 calculates overdue days using SYSDATE - due_date, but the schema has no column to record fine amounts or payment status. Overdue logic exists in queries but not in the data model — this is an incomplete design that would require schema changes before production use.

Action Items

✓ Recommendations

→ Acquire additional copies of the three fully unavailable titles
The Shining (2 reservations), Murder on the Orient Express, and War and Peace each have active reservation queues with zero copies on the shelf. A single additional copy of each would clear the backlog immediately. War and Peace is especially risky, one copy means any loss permanently removes the title.
→ Extend the loans table to support fine calculation
Add a fine_per_day NUMBER DEFAULT 0.50 and a fine_paid VARCHAR2(3) DEFAULT 'NO' column to the loans table. This would make Query 11 actionable — turning an analytical query into an operational tool for library staff to manage overdue collections. The analytical foundation already exists; only the schema needs extending.
